Learning to lead on and off the field

I never woke up one day and decided, “I’m a leader now.” It happened slowly: through sweaty practice sessions, cracked voices in the mic, and roll calls where I was responsible for every name. This page is a notebook of those moments—where I made mistakes, took responsibility, and slowly learned to stand in front of a group without running away.

Captaining a Kho Kho team

Kho Kho was the first place where I felt the weight of leadership on my shoulders. I wasn’t just running and dodging anymore—I was reading faces, calming nerves, and calling decisions that could change the scoreboard. There were matches where our strategy failed, where I misread the game and we lost by a single point. Those were the evenings I stayed back on the ground, replaying each move in my head, promising myself that next time I would think clearer and guide my team better.

Leading inside the classroom

Being a class leader taught me a different kind of courage. It wasn’t about shouting over noise; it was about earning quiet respect. I learned how to listen to complaints without taking sides, how to remind friends about deadlines without sounding like a teacher, and how to admit when I had passed on information incorrectly. Leadership here meant being reliable—showing up early, staying back late, and standing between the teacher and the class when something went wrong.

Holding a mic, holding a team together

On the music side, leadership looked less like giving commands and more like keeping everyone in rhythm. During school events, I coordinated practice schedules, balanced different skill levels, and made sure the shy voices in the back row were heard. When the sound system failed five minutes before a performance, I had to keep the team calm, speak to the teachers, and help reset everything in time. Those messy backstage moments taught me how to stay composed when things go off-script.

Calling commands for 50+ marching students

During March Past, I was responsible for leading a platoon of more than 50–70 students. The first time I stood at the front, my voice shook more than my legs, but hundreds of eyes were still waiting for my command. I learned how to project my voice, pace my instructions, and keep the formation steady even when someone fell out of step. Leading a line of students across the ground taught me that confidence is often just responsibility plus practice.

What these roles taught me about real leadership

Across sports, music, and academics, the pattern stayed the same: leadership wasn’t a trophy or a title pinned on my chest. It was taking the first step when others were unsure, accepting blame when plans failed, and quietly giving credit when things went right. These experiences now guide the way I want to lead in future teams, tech projects, and college communities—by being the person who listens deeply, decides clearly, and keeps the group moving forward.
